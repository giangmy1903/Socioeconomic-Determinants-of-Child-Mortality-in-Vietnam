# Socioeconomic Determinants of Child Mortality in Vietnam

The research applied multiple linear regression models with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors to evaluate the relationship between income, healthcare expenditure, access to hygienic water, and regional child mortality outcomes, and developed a set of recommendations tailored to each region to improve health outcomes and reduce the child mortality rate.  

## Research Question

Despite substantial economic growth over the past several decades, significant disparities in child health outcomes persist across regions of Vietnam. This project examines a central policy question: _Which socioeconomic factors have the greatest impact on reducing child mortality, and where should limited public resources be directed to generate the largest improvements in children's health?_ Understanding these relationships is critical for policymakers seeking to improve healthcare access, reduce regional inequalities, and allocate resources more effectively.

## Data & Methodology

Constructed separate cross-sectional multiple linear regression models for **Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)** and **Under-Five Mortality Rate (U5MR)** using regional socioeconomic indicators from Vietnam.

The baseline specification estimated the relationship between mortality outcomes and:

* Monthly income per capita
* Average healthcare expenditure
* Access to hygienic water sources

To improve inference reliability, heteroskedasticity diagnostics were performed, and robust standard errors were incorporated where appropriate. Statistical significance testing was used to evaluate whether observed relationships were distinguishable from random variation and suitable for policy recommendations.

## Key Findings
* Income matters most for infant health
* Healthcare spending alone is not a universal solution
* Clean water plays a significant role in child survival
* One policy does not fit all

## Policy Implications

The analysis suggests several priorities for policymakers:
 * Expand access to clean water infrastructure in regions with elevated under-five mortality.
 * Strengthen maternal and infant healthcare services in economically disadvantaged communities.
 * Use regional socioeconomic indicators to target interventions where resources can generate the greatest impact.
 * Design age-specific public health programs that address the distinct factors affecting infant and child mortality.

Rather than treating child mortality as a single issue, the findings suggest a more targeted approach that recognizes the different challenges children face at different stages of development in different regions of the country.
